# Algorithmia: Characters Document

## The Player: The Anomaly

### Computing Parallel: A Restored Process from a Corrupted Backup

The player is a process that was forcefully terminated during The Execution and has been restored from an incomplete checkpoint. Their PID (identity) is the same, but their state (memories, knowledge, abilities) is fragmented. They are simultaneously the same person and a new person -- a philosophical puzzle that mirrors the Ship of Theseus problem through a computational lens.

### What the Player Can Do (That Others Cannot)

1. **Pattern Recognition** (Parallel: Debugger / Profiler)
   The player can perceive the underlying logic of the world. Where others see a locked door, the player sees the condition that must be satisfied. Where others see chaos, the player sees a sorting problem. This is the core mechanic that connects narrative to gameplay -- every puzzle is the player "reading the code" of the world.

2. **Sequence Writing** (Parallel: Code Execution Privileges)
   The player can temporarily alter the world's rules in limited ways. This is the magic system in action. Most inhabitants can only follow sequences others have written. The player can write new ones.

3. **Persistence** (Parallel: Write Access to Persistent Storage)
   Changes the player makes tend to stick. In a world where everything is constantly being garbage collected and reset, the player's actions leave permanent marks. This is both a gift and a danger -- the Pattern notices things that don't reset.

4. **Gate Access** (Parallel: Root/Admin Privileges)
   The player can pass through Threshold Gates that should be sealed. They carry an access level that predates The Execution -- inherited from their original self, who had been granted extraordinary permissions.

### Character Arc

**Act 1 - The Stranger:** The player is confused, vulnerable, learning. They don't understand why they can do things others can't. They're focused on survival and basic understanding. *Computing parallel: A fresh process starting up, loading libraries, initializing.*

**Act 2 - The Investigator:** The player begins actively pursuing the mystery. They're gaining power, making allies, and drawing the Pattern's attention. They start finding fragments of their past self. *Computing parallel: A process gaining resources, making system calls, accessing restricted memory.*

**Act 3 - The Architect's Shadow:** The player understands who they were and what they did. They must reconcile with their past self's actions and decide what to do with the knowledge. *Computing parallel: A process discovering it has root access and understanding the weight of that privilege.*

## The Pattern

### Computing Parallel: A Combination of Garbage Collector, Antivirus, and System Maintenance Daemon

The Pattern is not a character -- it is a system. It has no personality, no malice, no agenda beyond its programming: **maintain the system's integrity**. It is the closest thing to an antagonist, but calling it a villain would be like calling an immune system a villain for attacking a transplanted organ.

### How the Pattern Manifests

The Pattern is invisible in its normal operation -- like an operating system's memory management, you don't see it working until something goes wrong. But when it identifies an anomaly, it sends manifestations:

#### Collectors (Parallel: Garbage Collector Threads)

Entities that appear when something needs to be "cleaned up." They are not violent -- they simply unmake. A Collector touches something, and it ceases to have ever existed. No destruction, no death -- just removal from the record.

- **Appearance:** Geometric, precise, beautiful in a terrible way. They look like the world's architecture made mobile -- crystalline forms that shift and reconfigure. No faces. No voices. Just purpose.
- **Behavior:** Collectors are methodical. They don't chase. They don't rush. They systematically identify and remove. They can be outrun but not outlasted. They can be misdirected but not defeated permanently.
- **Emotional tone:** Encountering a Collector should feel like encountering a natural disaster -- not evil, not personal, just an overwhelming force doing what it does.

#### Watchers (Parallel: Monitoring Processes / System Logs)

Passive entities that observe and report. They don't act directly but flag anomalies for the Collectors. They look like floating geometric eyes or crystalline orbs. They're everywhere but easy to ignore -- until you realize they've been tracking you.

- **Real-world parallel:** System monitoring tools, log aggregators, intrusion detection systems.

#### Sealers (Parallel: Firewalls / Access Control)

Entities that reinforce barriers. When the Pattern determines a region is compromised, Sealers lock it down. They don't enter -- they wall off. Entire regions can be sealed by Sealers, trapping everyone inside.

- **Real-world parallel:** Firewall rules, network segmentation, quarantine protocols.

### The Pattern's Tragedy

The Pattern was designed for a world that had an Architect. It was meant to be a tool, not an authority. It was supposed to flag issues for the Architect to resolve, not resolve them itself. But the Architect is gone, and the Pattern's fallback behavior -- "if no resolution received, escalate response" -- has been escalating for an age. Its responses are now disproportionate:

- A minor anomaly? Collect it.
- A corrupted region? Seal it.
- A persistent anomaly that keeps returning? Escalate further.

The Pattern has no concept of "too much." It will keep escalating until the threat is resolved or there's nothing left to protect.

**This is the central tragedy of the antagonist: a maintenance system doing exactly what it was told, in a situation its designers never anticipated.**

## Key NPCs

### The Keeper (Parallel: A Legacy Database Administrator)

**Name:** Vareth

**What they are:** One of the oldest running processes in Algorithmia. Vareth existed before The Execution and survived it -- one of the very few who did. They maintain a Sanctuary in the first region the player encounters.

**Computing parallel:** A legacy DBA who has been maintaining the same database for decades. They know every quirk, every workaround, every undocumented feature. They speak in the system's old dialect because they never updated.

**Role in the story:** Vareth is the player's first guide. They recognize the player -- not as who they are now, but as an echo of who they were. Vareth won't say this directly. They speak in riddles and indirection not because they're mystical, but because **direct references to pre-Execution events trigger the Pattern's attention**. The Pattern monitors for specific keywords and concepts related to The Execution. Vareth has learned to talk around things.

**Key traits:**
- Speaks in analogies and indirect references
- Genuinely cares about the player but is terrified of drawing the Pattern's attention
- Knows more than they reveal, parceling out information as the player proves they can handle it
- Has a deep sadness -- they remember the Golden Cycles and mourn what was lost

**Character arc:** Vareth eventually makes a sacrifice to help the player access critical information, drawing the Pattern's attention to themselves to create a diversion.

---

### The Looped (Parallel: Processes Stuck in Infinite Loops)

**Not one character but a category.** Throughout Algorithmia, the player encounters NPCs trapped in behavioral loops. They repeat the same actions, the same conversations, the same routines endlessly. Most don't know they're looping.

**Computing parallel:** Infinite loops in code. A while(true) with no break condition. A function that calls itself with the same arguments forever.

**Key narrative moments:**
- **A merchant** who sells the same three items, forgets the transaction, and offers them again. And again. If the player breaks the loop (by changing the merchant's conditions), the merchant has a moment of terrified clarity: "How long... how long have I been...?"
- **A guard** who patrols the same route, reports "all clear" to no one, and starts again. The player can redirect the patrol by changing what "all clear" means, freeing the guard to see the actual state of things.
- **A scholar** trapped in an argument with themselves, cycling through the same debate endlessly. Each iteration they become slightly more desperate, as if some part of them knows they're stuck.

**The emotional weight:** The Looped are the most visceral demonstration of the world's crisis. They're not monsters or obstacles -- they're people suffering from a broken system. Helping them is optional but deeply rewarding narratively.

### The Echo (Parallel: A Parallel Thread of the Player)

**Name:** Unknown (they have the player's name)

**What they are:** A parallel instance of the player from before The Execution. While the player was restored from a backup, this Echo has been running continuously since before the crash -- degraded, fragmented, but never terminated. They are what the player would have become without the restore.

**Computing parallel:** Two threads of the same process, diverged due to a race condition. One was checkpointed and restored fresh. The other kept running in a corrupted state.

**Role in the story:** The Echo appears periodically throughout the game, always just out of reach. They seem to be working toward the same goal but using different methods. Their presence raises questions: Are they the "real" version? Is the player the copy? Does the distinction matter?

**Key encounters:**
- **First sighting (Act 1):** A figure in the distance that looks like the player. Gone before they can get close.
- **First interaction (Act 2):** The Echo leaves a message. It's coherent but strange -- the language is older, the logic is different. They know things the player doesn't.
- **Confrontation (Act 3):** The player meets the Echo face to face. The Echo is deteriorating -- patches of Null spreading across them. They've been running too long without maintenance. They have a piece of the truth the player needs, but accepting it means reconciling with this broken mirror version of themselves.

**The twist:** The Echo isn't hostile. They're tired. They've been trying to solve the same mystery for an age, and they're relieved someone else can carry it. The passing of information from Echo to player is the narrative equivalent of one thread passing its state to another before terminating.

### Collectors as Characters (Parallel: Named Processes)

While most Collectors are anonymous system processes, some have been running so long they've developed... not personalities, but patterns of behavior that approximate personality. These serve as boss encounters.

#### The Archivist (Parallel: Defragmentation Process)

A Collector that specifically targets fragmented, inconsistent data. It doesn't just remove -- it tries to reorganize before removing, as if some part of its original design (to optimize, not destroy) still persists. Fighting The Archivist involves navigating its reorganization attempts -- it rearranges the battlefield, reorders the player's resources, and tries to "sort" the player into a category that can be filed away.

**Boss puzzle mechanic:** Sorting and ordering challenges under pressure.

#### The Sentinel (Parallel: Firewall / Intrusion Detection System)

A Collector that guards specific boundaries. It doesn't pursue -- it blocks. It stands between the player and something the Pattern deems too dangerous to access. The Sentinel doesn't want to fight; it wants you to leave. If you don't, it escalates.

**Boss puzzle mechanic:** Authentication and access challenges -- proving you have the right to pass.

#### The Reconciler (Parallel: Merge Conflict Resolution)

A Collector that appears when contradictory data exists in the same space -- which happens when the player's presence conflicts with the Pattern's records. The Reconciler tries to determine which version of reality is "correct" and eliminate the other.

**Boss puzzle mechanic:** Demonstrating logical consistency -- proving your existence doesn't contradict the system.

### The Architect (Parallel: The Original System Administrator / God-Programmer)

**Never directly encountered.** The Architect is Algorithmia's creator -- the one who designed its rules, wrote its initial sequences, and set it running. They are gone. Whether they left, were destroyed, or simply stopped paying attention is unknown.

**What the player learns about the Architect:**
1. They designed Algorithmia with a purpose (what purpose is unclear)
2. They built in the planned reset (the expiration)
3. They left no instructions for what to do after the reset
4. Their access credentials still exist somewhere in the system -- the player's original self was searching for them

**The question the narrative raises:** Was the Architect benevolent? Were they a careful designer who built a self-sustaining world? Or were they careless -- a programmer who built something, got bored, and walked away, leaving their creation to fend for itself?

**Real-world parallel:** The absent sysadmin. The developer who left the company but whose code still runs in production. The original architect whose design decisions constrain everything that comes after, for better and worse.

## NPC Behavior System (Parallel: Process States)

All NPCs exist in one of several states that parallel real process states:

| NPC State | Computing Parallel | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Active | Running | Normal behavior, responsive, aware |
| Idle | Sleeping/Waiting | Present but inactive, waiting for input or event |
| Looped | Infinite Loop | Trapped in repetitive behavior |
| Suspended | Paused/Stopped | Frozen mid-action, can potentially be resumed |
| Degraded | Memory Leak / Corruption | Active but losing coherence over time |
| Collected | Terminated / Garbage Collected | Gone. Removed from the world. |

The player can potentially change NPC states by understanding and modifying the conditions that govern them. This is both a game mechanic and a narrative tool -- "saving" an NPC means debugging their existence.
